Straight answers

The questions
I’d ask too.

You have a full-time job. Will my fix get squeezed out?

No. A sprint runs in a scheduled window with a hard eight-hour cap, and I take very few of them. If I can’t give a problem a real window, I don’t take it. The job cuts the other way too: you’re hiring someone who writes software for a living, all week, every week.

What if my problem is bigger than one sprint?

You hear that up front, in the fit decision, before any payment. The options stay simple: a $200 diagnostic to pin down the cause, a $900 extended sprint agreed in writing, or “not a fit” with my reasoning.

What if you can’t fix it?

Then I shouldn’t have accepted it, and the fit decision exists to catch that. I only take problems I’m confident fit the cap. If diagnosis changes the picture mid-sprint, you get my findings in writing immediately and decide the next step. Hours never quietly pile up.

Why pay a 60% deposit to someone you found on the internet?

Because by the time money moves, you’re not buying a promise, you’re approving a document. The written scope comes first: symptom, definition of done, price, exclusions. The $360 deposit reserves a scheduled window, the $240 balance is due only when you accept the work, and the $200 diagnostic exists if you want a smaller first step.

Do we need a call?

No. The whole flow is written: your form, my scope, the delivery notes. Expect replies in the evening and on weekends, in writing. If you’d rather talk one through, ask.

Can this be white-label for your client work?

Yes. I can work quietly in your repository, deliver the fix as a pull request, and let your team own the client relationship.
